Tumor Necrosis Factor

TNF

The tumour necrosis factor (TNF) gene, located on chromosome 6, encodes a multifunctional proinflammatory cytokine, the TNF-α , which is a member of the TNF superfamily. 

Furthermore, it plays a crucial role in regulating a broad range of biological processes (including cell proliferation, differentiation, apoptosis, lipid metabolism, and coagulation).

tnf Mutations

Dysregulation or mutations in the TNF gene have been linked to a wide range of diseases, including autoimmune disorders, chronic inflammatory conditions, infectious diseases, and even cancer. TNF-α, the protein product of this gene, plays a dual role - it can promote inflammation and apoptosis in certain contexts while also contributing to cell proliferation and survival in others. Due to its central role in immune regulation, both excessive and insufficient TNF-α activity can lead to pathological conditions.


Autoimmune and inflammatory diseases

In rheumatoid arthritis (RA), excessive TNF-α production leads to chronic joint inflammation and tissue damage. Similarly, in inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), including Crohn’s disease and ulcerative colitis, elevated TNF-α levels drive intestinal inflammation. Psoriasis and psoriatic arthritis are also linked to TNF-α overactivity, where the cytokine contributes to skin lesions and joint degeneration. These diseases are commonly treated with anti-TNF biologics, which have revolutionized management but come with risks like increased susceptibility to infections.


Infectious diseases and sepsis

While it is essential for fighting infections, excessive TNF-α can trigger a cytokine storm, leading to septic shock and multi-organ failure. 

Certain genetic polymorphisms, such as TNF-308G>A, have been associated with higher sepsis risk. 


Cancer development and progression

In some cancers, such as lymphomas (e.g., non-Hodgkin lymphoma), certain TNF polymorphisms (e.g., TNF-308A) are associated with increased risk. 

TNF-α can promote tumor growth by enhancing angiogenesis and suppressing anti-tumor immunity, yet in other contexts, it can induce cancer cell death. This dual role makes TNF-α a challenging but intriguing target in oncology.


Neurodegenerative diseases

In neurodegenerative diseases, TNF-α contributes to neuroinflammation, which is implicated in conditions like Alzheimer’s disease (AD) and multiple sclerosis (MS). In AD, elevated TNF-α levels may accelerate amyloid plaque formation and neuronal damage. In MS, TNF-α is involved in demyelination, though paradoxically, some anti-TNF therapies have been found to worsen the disease, underscoring the complexity of TNF signaling in the nervous system.


Genetic polymorphisms

Genetic polymorphisms in the TNF gene further influence disease susceptibility and severity. Variants such as TNF-308G>A, TNF-238G>A, and TNF-857C>T have been linked to altered TNF-α production and increased risk for diseases like lupus, asthma, and malaria. 


The development of anti-TNF therapies has been a major breakthrough in treating autoimmune diseases, but their use requires careful monitoring due to potential side effects, including infections, lymphoma, and heart complications.  

Overall, the TNF gene and its protein product remain central to understanding and treating a wide array of diseases, making it a critical focus of biomedical research.
